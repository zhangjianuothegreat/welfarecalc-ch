💡 Did you know? Rent increases in Switzerland are cost-based. That means the landlord may only increase the rent if their actual costs have increased – e.g., due to rising interest rates (reference interest rate), inflation, or value-enhancing investments.

🏠 What is the maximum rent increase?

📄 Typical landlord demand (often problematic):
"The rent will be increased by 15% to CHF 1,495 as of May 1, 2026."

⚠️ The truth: There is no fixed percentage limit for rent increases in Switzerland! An increase is only permissible if it is covered by concrete cost increases of the landlord (Art. 269a CO).

Three main reasons allow an increase:

  • Reference interest rate rises – For every 0.25% increase, the landlord may charge max. 3% more rent
  • Inflation – Max. 40% of the CPI increase can be passed on
  • Value-enhancing investments – After renovations that increase the living value
Example: Your rent is CHF 1,200. The reference interest rate rises from 1.25% to 1.50% (+0.25%). The landlord may charge a maximum of 3% more, i.e., CHF 1,236. An increase to CHF 1,380 (+15%) would not be legal without additional reasons (such as inflation or renovation).

✅ What you should do: Ask the landlord for a detailed breakdown of the calculation! Only with concrete figures can you check whether the increase is lawful.

📅 How often is a rent increase allowed?

📄 Inadmissible clause (occurs):
"The landlord may adjust the rent at any change of the reference interest rate, even multiple times per year."

⚠️ Why dangerous? According to Art. 269d CO, there must be a minimum interval of 12 months between two rent increases. Even if the reference interest rate changes several times a year, the landlord may not increase every quarter.

🧒 Plain language: Your parents cannot say "you pay more pocket money" every month. At least one year must pass between two increases – otherwise it's unfair.

✅ Important: If the reference interest rate falls, as a tenant you have the right to demand a reduction – the landlord cannot use this period for an increase.

👴 Rent increase for long-term tenants (>10 years)

📄 Common misconception:
"Long-term tenants are automatically entitled to lower increases (5-8%)."

⚖️ The legal reality: There is no special statutory rule that automatically grants long-term tenants lower percentage limits. However, long-term tenants benefit from the "relative method" (BGE 124 III 67, BGE 133 III 61).

What does this mean for you?

  • The landlord cannot simply raise the rent to the current market level
  • They may only pass on the actual cost increases since the last adjustment
  • "Local custom" alone is not a sufficient reason for an increase in existing rental contracts
💡 Tip for long-term tenants: The landlord must meet particularly high evidentiary hurdles (e.g., comprehensive modernization with detailed cost breakdown). A simple "adjustment to local custom" is not sufficient for you.

✅ Which reasons for a rent increase are allowed?

Permitted (Art. 269a CO):

  • 📈 Increase in the reference interest rate – The current reference interest rate is 1.25% (as of June 2026, last adjustment September 2025). For every 0.25% increase: max. +3% rent possible.
  • 📊 General inflation – The landlord may pass on a maximum of 40% of the CPI increase since the last adjustment.
  • 🔧 Increased maintenance costs – If heating, water, janitor etc. become more expensive (must be provable).
  • 🏗️ Value-enhancing investments – After renovations that actually increase the living value (e.g., new bathroom, better windows). Pure maintenance does not count.
  • 🏘️ Local and neighborhood custom – Only under strict conditions: The landlord must provide evidence of at least 5 comparable properties in the same neighborhood with the same features.

Not allowed:

  • ❌ "Because I as a landlord want more money" – not a valid reason
  • ❌ "The neighboring apartment was rented more expensively" – alone not sufficient
  • ❌ Retaliation after a defect notification or complaint
  • ❌ Because the landlord took out a new loan with higher interest rates – does not count! Only the official reference interest rate counts.
⚠️ Caution: The landlord must state the reason in writing for each rent increase and disclose the calculation. If this is missing or the official cantonal form is not used, the increase is contestable!

❓ Frequently asked questions about rent increases (FAQ)

Can my landlord increase the rent every year?
No, an increase is possible at the earliest 12 months after the last permissible increase (Art. 269d CO). In addition, a legal reason under Art. 269a CO must exist.
Can the landlord increase the rent due to personal use?
Personal use is not a reason for a rent increase – it can only lead to termination, but not to an increase of the existing rent.
What is the maximum rent increase after a renovation?
Value-enhancing investments can be passed on to the rent, but the total burden must not be abusive. There is no fixed percentage limit – the increase must be based on the actual, provable investment costs (usually 50-70% of costs are recognized as value-enhancing).
What is the difference between net rent and gross rent?
Net rent = pure rent without ancillary costs; gross rent = net rent + ancillary costs (heating, water, janitor). Rent increases usually refer to the net rent. Ancillary costs can be adjusted separately if actual costs rise.
Can I terminate the lease after a rent increase?
Yes, you have an extraordinary right of termination if the increase is unlawful. The notice period is usually the next statutory date (usually 3 months).
What is the current reference interest rate (2026)?
The current reference interest rate is 1.25% (as of June 2026). The last adjustment was made in September 2025 from 1.50% to 1.25%. An increase in the reference interest rate would allow the landlord to adjust the rent – for every 0.25% increase max. +3%.
